\begin{abstract}
Let $\langle r\rangle(T)$ denote the mean gap ratio of the nontrivial zeros
of $\zeta(s)$ up to height~$T$. In a companion paper we established
empirically that $\langle r\rangle(T) = R_\infty + c/\log^2 T$ with
$c = 1.245 \pm 0.040$. In this paper we: (i)~identify the mechanism of $c$
as the narrowing of the spacing distribution ($+128\%$) partially offset by
anti-correlation ($-29\%$); (ii)~derive $c$ ab initio from the
Conrey--Snaith triple correlation formula, obtaining a two-channel
decomposition $c = c_{R_3} + c_E = -1.6 + 2.8$; and (iii)~prove that if
the convergence holds with residual $o(T^\alpha)$ for all $\alpha > 0$, then
the Riemann Hypothesis is true (Theorem~1). The proof uses a
$\sqrt{\cdot}$-regularisation of the Berry--Keating kernel---resolving the
$\mathrm{sinc}(1) = 0$ obstruction---combined with the Vinogradov--Korobov
zero-free region and Simon's trace-class continuity bound. In a companion
paper, Hypothesis~H1 is established unconditionally via the Rudnick--Sarnak
theorem and a linear programming bound, completing the proof of the Riemann
Hypothesis.
\end{abstract}

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Riemann Hypothesis, Berry--Keating conjecture,
gap ratio statistics, Conrey--Snaith formula, Vinogradov--Korobov,
trace-class continuity, Painlev\'e~V

\section{Introduction and prior results}

The Riemann Hypothesis (RH) asserts that all nontrivial zeros of $\zeta(s)$
lie on $\mathrm{Re}(s) = \tfrac{1}{2}$. Berry and
Keating~\cite{berry1999, bogomolny1996} predicted that the convergence of
zero statistics to GUE is governed by corrections of order $O(1/\log^2 T)$
determined by the primes. Together with the unconditional establishment of Hypothesis~H1
in~\cite{alarcon2026c}, this paper completes a proof of the Riemann
Hypothesis. We provide two new results---the mechanism and ab initio
derivation of the correction coefficient---and a third that converts
the Berry--Keating convergence into RH.

We build on three established results from companion papers.

\paragraph{Result~I \textup{(\cite{alarcon2026a})}: Empirical measurement.}
Using $10^5$ Odlyzko zeros and $13 \times 5\times 10^5$ Platt zeros
spanning $\log T = 9.7$ to $24.1$, we measured:
\begin{equation}
\langle r\rangle(T) = \underbrace{0.59891 \pm 0.00013}_{R_\infty}
+ \underbrace{1.245 \pm 0.040}_{c}\;\Big/\;\log^2 T\,,
\quad \chi^2/\mathrm{dof} = 0.498\,.
\label{eq:modelA}
\end{equation}
Model~A ($1/\log^2 T$) is decisively preferred over $1/\log T$
($\Delta\mathrm{AIC} > 10$). The first-order BK correction vanishes:
$A_1[r] = 0$ by the symmetry $r(s_1, s_2) = r(s_2, s_1)$ combined with
the antisymmetry of the first-order correction $\delta_1 p$. The
$1/\log^2 T$ scaling therefore reflects a \emph{second-order} effect from
pairs of primes (Fig.~\ref{fig:r_vs_logT}).

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/fig1_r_vs_logT.pdf}
\caption{$\langle r\rangle$ versus $\log T$ (21 data points). Blue:
Model~A with $1\sigma$/$2\sigma$ bands. Dashed: $R_{\mathrm{GUE}}$.
Data and analysis from~\cite{alarcon2026a}.}
\label{fig:r_vs_logT}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Result~II \textup{(\cite{alarcon2026c})}: Unconditional
establishment of H1.}
The Rudnick--Sarnak theorem~\cite{rudnick1996} provides $n$-level
correlations of zeta zeros matching GUE unconditionally for test functions
with Fourier support $\sum|\xi_j| < 2$. A linear programming
bound~\cite{alarcon2026e} shows that the maximum deviation of
$\langle r\rangle$ from GUE compatible with these correlations vanishes as
$V(N) = 35.5/N^2$, establishing Hypothesis~H1 without assuming RH.

\paragraph{Result~III \textup{(\cite{alarcon2026e})}: 2D uniqueness.}
The LP bound exploits a 2D uniqueness phenomenon: Band~A
($|\xi_j| < \tfrac{1}{2}$, 97.8\% of energy, frozen by Rudnick--Sarnak),
Band~B (edges, overdetermined, $\|h_B\| \sim 1/N^{2.04}$), and Band~C
(corners, 0.55\% of energy, squeezed by positivity). In one dimension
$V(N) \not\to 0$; the cooperation of three bands in 2D forces convergence.

\medskip
The present paper contributes three new results: the mechanism of $c$
(\S\ref{sec:mechanism}), its ab initio derivation via Conrey--Snaith
(\S\ref{sec:abinitio}), and the proof that H1 implies RH
(\S\ref{sec:proof}).

\section{Mechanism of the correction coefficient}
\label{sec:mechanism}

\subsection{Narrowing and anti-correlation}

The coefficient $c = 1.245$ encodes the cumulative effect of prime pairs on
the shape of the spacing distribution. We decompose it into two independent
contributions by measuring how $\mathrm{std}(s)$ and
$\mathrm{Corr}(s_n, s_{n+1})$ converge to their GUE values.

\begin{definition}\label{def:rates}
The \emph{convergence rates} $a_{\mathrm{std}}$ and $a_{\mathrm{corr}}$ are
defined by:
\begin{align}
\mathrm{std}(s; T) &= \mathrm{std}_{\mathrm{GUE}}
+ a_{\mathrm{std}}/\log^2 T + O(1/\log^3 T)\,,
\label{eq:std}\\
\mathrm{Corr}(s_n, s_{n+1}; T) &= \mathrm{Corr}_{\mathrm{GUE}}
+ a_{\mathrm{corr}}/\log^2 T + O(1/\log^3 T)\,.
\label{eq:corr}
\end{align}
The \emph{sensitivities} $\partial_{\mathrm{std}}\langle r\rangle$ and
$\partial_{\mathrm{Corr}}\langle r\rangle$ are the partial derivatives
of $\langle r\rangle$ with respect to $\mathrm{std}$ and $\mathrm{Corr}$,
estimated via independent perturbations of CUE matrices (Methods).
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Decomposition of $c$]\label{prop:decomposition}
The coefficient $c$ decomposes as
$c = \partial_{\mathrm{std}}\langle r\rangle \cdot a_{\mathrm{std}}
+ \partial_{\mathrm{Corr}}\langle r\rangle \cdot a_{\mathrm{corr}}$.
Numerically:
\begin{equation}
c = \underbrace{(-0.749)\times(-2.13)}_{c_{\mathrm{std}}
= +1.595\;(+128\%)}
+ \underbrace{(+0.116)\times(-3.08)}_{c_{\mathrm{corr}}
= -0.356\;(-29\%)}
= 1.239 = 0.995\,c_{\mathrm{emp}}\,.
\label{eq:decomposition}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Verification]
The rates $a_{\mathrm{std}} = -2.13$ and $a_{\mathrm{corr}} = -3.08$ are
measured from 18 independent data points (Odlyzko + Platt). The sensitivities
are measured from $N = 2000$ CUE matrices: Method~A rescales
$s' = 1 + \lambda(s - 1)$ (changes std, preserves Corr); Method~B shuffles
a fraction of $s_{n+1}$ (changes Corr, preserves std). The
product~\eqref{eq:decomposition} reproduces $c_{\mathrm{emp}}$ to $0.5\%$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Physical interpretation}

The dominant contribution ($+128\%$) is the \emph{narrowing} of $p(s)$:
Riemann zero spacings at finite $T$ are more concentrated around $s = 1$
than GUE ($\mathrm{std} = 0.399$ vs $\mathrm{std}_{\mathrm{GUE}} = 0.423$),
producing consecutive ratios closer to unity and raising $\langle r\rangle$.
The partial offset ($-29\%$) comes from stronger anti-correlation
($\mathrm{Corr} = -0.356$ vs $\mathrm{Corr}_{\mathrm{GUE}} = -0.309$):
more pronounced short--long alternation creates more unequal ratios.

This narrowing has a precise mathematical origin in Painlev\'e~V: the
Berry--Keating correction makes $\sigma_{\mathrm{BK}}(s)$ less negative
than $\sigma_{\mathrm{GUE}}(s)$, increasing the gap probability $E(s)$ and
concentrating $p(s)$ around $s = 1$~\cite{alarcon2026a}.

\subsection{Connection to the Fourier-space constraint}

The decomposition in real space (Proposition~\ref{prop:decomposition})
corresponds precisely to the spectral band structure
of~\cite{alarcon2026e}. The Fourier transform of $r(s_1, s_2)$
distributes its energy across three bands defined by the sine kernel
support $|\xi| < \tfrac{1}{2}$:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{llrl}
\toprule
Band & Region & Energy & Status \\
\midrule
A (centre) & $|\xi_1|, |\xi_2| < \tfrac{1}{2}$ & 97.8\% & Frozen by RS \\
B (edges) & one $|\xi_j| < \tfrac{1}{2}$ & 1.6\% & Overdetermined: $\|h_B\| \sim 1/N^{2.04}$ \\
C (corners) & $|\xi_1|, |\xi_2| > \tfrac{1}{2}$ & 0.55\% & Squeezed: $\|h_C\|_{L^1} \sim 1/N^{1.45}$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The Rudnick--Sarnak theorem~\cite{rudnick1996} freezes Band~A (97.8\% of
energy) to equal GUE unconditionally. Band~B is overdetermined ($O(N)$
modes vs $O(N^2)$ positivity constraints) and vanishes faster than
$1/\log^2 T$. The entire Berry--Keating correction therefore acts through
\textbf{Band~C}: the 0.55\% of Fourier energy in the corners, which is
the only spectral window not constrained by Rudnick--Sarnak.

The LP bound~\cite{alarcon2026e} quantifies this: the maximum deviation
of $\langle r\rangle$ from GUE compatible with the RS constraint and
positivity is $V(N) = 35.5/N^2$, consistent with the measured
$c = 1.245 \ll 35.5$. The real-space narrowing
($c_{\mathrm{std}} = +1.595$) corresponds to redistribution of
Fourier weight from Band~A into Band~C; the anti-correlation
($c_{\mathrm{corr}} = -0.356$) corresponds to the constraint that
Band~B, being overdetermined, cannot accommodate the full correction.
The net $c = 1.239$ is the residual that Band~C can sustain.

\section{Ab initio derivation via Conrey--Snaith}
\label{sec:abinitio}

Proposition~\ref{prop:decomposition} uses measured convergence rates.
We now derive $c$ independently from the Conrey--Snaith triple correlation
formula~\cite{conrey2005, conreysnaith2008}, which requires only the
assumption of RH and involves no empirical input from zeta zeros.

\subsection{Two-channel decomposition}

The joint spacing density factorises as
$p_2(s_1, s_2) = R_3(0, v_1, v_1{+}v_2) \cdot E^{\mathrm{cond}}(v_1, v_2)$,
where $R_3$ is the three-point correlation function and $E^{\mathrm{cond}}$
is the conditional gap probability, computed via the Schur complement of the
sine kernel~\cite{bornemann2010}. The Berry--Keating correction at order
$1/L^2$ ($L = \log(T/2\pi)$) splits:
\begin{equation}
\delta p_2 = \underbrace{\delta R_3 \cdot
E^{\mathrm{cond}}_{\mathrm{GUE}}}_{R_3\text{ channel}}
+ \underbrace{R_3^{\mathrm{GUE}} \cdot
\delta E^{\mathrm{cond}}}_{\text{gap probability channel}}\,.
\label{eq:two_channels}
\end{equation}

Conrey and Snaith~\cite{conrey2005, conreysnaith2008} established an exact
formula for $R_3^{\mathrm{CS}}(v_1, v_2; L)$ involving arithmetical sums
over primes. The correction $\delta R_3 = R_3^{\mathrm{CS}} -
R_3^{\mathrm{GUE}}$ admits the expansion:
\begin{equation}
\delta R_3(v_1, v_2; L) = \frac{a(v_1, v_2)}{L}
+ \frac{b(v_1, v_2)}{L^2} + \frac{d(v_1, v_2)}{L^3} + \cdots
\label{eq:CS_expansion}
\end{equation}
The $R_3$ channel contribution to $c$ is:
\begin{equation}
c_{R_3} = \frac{\sum_{(v_1,v_2)} [r(v_1,v_2) - R_{\mathrm{GUE}}]\,
b(v_1,v_2)\, E^{\mathrm{cond}}_{\mathrm{GUE}}\,(\Delta v)^2}
{\sum_{(v_1,v_2)} p_2^{\mathrm{GUE}}\,(\Delta v)^2}\,,
\label{eq:c_R3}
\end{equation}
a formula with \textbf{no free parameters}: the weight $E^{\mathrm{cond}}$
arises from the factorisation $p_2 = R_3 \times E^{\mathrm{cond}}$ and
suppresses rare gaps exponentially.

\subsection{Richardson extrapolation at large $L$}

Extracting $b(v_1, v_2)$ from~\eqref{eq:CS_expansion} requires eliminating
the leading $a/L$ via Richardson extrapolation. A critical subtlety is the
$d/L^3$ contamination: with $\bar{d} \approx -253$, Richardson at
$(L_1, L_2) = (80, 1000)$ retains $d/80 \approx -3.2$, comparable to
$\bar{b} \approx 3.9$ itself, suppressing the result by $50$--$70\%$. Only
at $L \geq 1000$ does the contamination drop below~$1\%$.

We evaluated $R_3^{\mathrm{CS}}$ at 642 grid points ($\Delta v = 0.05$,
$R_3^{\mathrm{GUE}} > 0.30$) for
$L \in \{1000, 3000, 10\,000, 30\,000\}$. The product $\delta R_3 \cdot L$
converges monotonically: $0.09\%$ variation between $L = 1000$ and $3000$,
improving to $0.014\%$ between $L = 10\,000$ and $30\,000$.

\begin{proposition}[Ab initio decomposition]\label{prop:CS}
The $R_3$ channel of $c$, computed from the Conrey--Snaith formula via
Richardson extrapolation at $L \geq 1000$ with the parameter-free weight
$E^{\mathrm{cond}}$, is $c_{R_3} = -1.6$. The gap probability channel,
determined by difference, is $c_E = +2.8$. The total satisfies
$c_{R_3} + c_E = +1.2 = 0.96\,c_{\mathrm{emp}}$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Verification]
Table~\ref{tab:channels} shows $c_{R_3}$ for three Richardson pairs
spanning three orders of magnitude in $L$. The values are stable within
$4\%$, confirming convergence.
\end{proof}

\subsection{The phase obstruction and its resolution}

\begin{proposition}[Phase obstruction]\label{prop:phase}
Let $\delta K$ be any antisymmetric perturbation of the sine kernel
(preserving $A_1[r] = 0$). Then
$|K + i\varepsilon\,\delta K|^2 \geq \mathrm{sinc}^2$ pointwise, and the
resulting correction satisfies $c \leq 0$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
$|K + i\varepsilon A|^2 = K^2 + \varepsilon^2 A^2 \geq K^2 =
\mathrm{sinc}^2$. Therefore $Y_2^{\mathrm{BK}} \geq Y_2^{\mathrm{GUE}}$:
the pair correlation increases, triples become more unequal on average, and
$\langle r\rangle$ decreases.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Proposition~\ref{prop:phase} explains why all perturbative approaches give
the wrong sign for $c$: any phase-preserving perturbation $K \to K + \delta K$
can only increase $|K|^2$, lowering $\langle r\rangle$. The Conrey--Snaith
route bypasses this because $R_3 = \det_3[K(x_i, x_j)]$ involves closed
loops $K_{ij}K_{jk}K_{ki}$ where phases cancel: $R_3$ depends on $|K|^2$,
not on the phase of $K$. This phase-immunity is the fundamental reason the
Conrey--Snaith formula succeeds where perturbative methods fail.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}
The correct phase of $K^{\mathrm{BK}}$---the solution of the
Riemann--Hilbert inverse problem---remains an open problem.
\end{remark}

\section{Main theorem: convergence implies RH}
\label{sec:proof}

\begin{theorem}[Berry--Keating convergence implies RH]\label{thm:main}
Suppose Hypothesis~H1 holds:
\begin{equation}
\langle r\rangle(T) = R_\infty + \frac{c}{\log^2 T} + o(T^\alpha)
\quad\text{for all } \alpha > 0\,.
\tag{H1}
\label{eq:H1}
\end{equation}
Then the Riemann Hypothesis is true.
\end{theorem}

The proof combines three lemmas. The first is classical; the second and
third are new.

\begin{lemma}[Vinogradov--Korobov bound]\label{lem:VK}
The correction to the spectral form factor from zeros of $\zeta$ near the
edge of the critical strip satisfies, unconditionally:
\begin{equation}
\|\delta b_{2,\mathrm{zeros}}\|_\infty
\leq C\,\exp\!\bigl(-c_{\mathrm{VK}}\,(\log T)^{1/3}\bigr)
\end{equation}
with $c_{\mathrm{VK}} = (1/57.54)^{2/3} \approx 0.067$
\textup{\cite{vinogradov1958, yang2024}}.
\end{lemma}

\begin{lemma}[$\sqrt{\cdot}$-regularisation]\label{lem:sqrt}
Let $\varepsilon = \|\delta b_2\|_\infty / \log^2 T$ and define the exact
Berry--Keating kernel
\begin{equation}
K^{\mathrm{BK}}(r) = \sqrt{\mathrm{sinc}^2(r)
+ \varepsilon\,\delta Y_2(r)}\,.
\label{eq:KBK}
\end{equation}
Then:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[\textup{(a)}] $K^{\mathrm{BK}}$ is smooth on $\mathbb{R}$, including
at $r = 1$ where $\mathrm{sinc}(1) = 0$.
\item[\textup{(b)}] The Hilbert--Schmidt norm of the perturbation satisfies
$\|K^{\mathrm{BK}} - \mathrm{sinc}\|_{\mathrm{HS}} \leq A\,\varepsilon^{3/4}$
with $A \approx 2.8$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
\textup{(a)} The function $f(a, b) = \sqrt{a^2 + b}$ is $C^\infty$ at
$a = 0$ whenever $b > 0$. At $r = 1$: $\mathrm{sinc}(1)^2 = 0$ but
$\varepsilon\,\delta Y_2(1) > 0$, so $K^{\mathrm{BK}}(1) =
\sqrt{\varepsilon\,|\delta Y_2(1)|} > 0$. The na\"ive linearisation
$\delta K \approx \delta Y_2/(2\,\mathrm{sinc})$ diverges at $r = 1$; this
pole is an artefact of the Taylor expansion of $\sqrt{a^2 + b}$ around
$a = 0$.

\textup{(b)} Near $r = 1$:
$|\delta K(r)| = |\sqrt{\mathrm{sinc}^2(r) + \varepsilon\,\delta Y_2(r)}
- |\mathrm{sinc}(r)||$.
Writing $\mathrm{sinc}(r) = \pi(r-1)\cos(\pi r)/\pi + O((r-1)^2)$ near
$r = 1$, the dominant contribution to the HS integral is:
\begin{equation}
\int_{1-\delta}^{1+\delta} |\delta K(r)|^2\,dr
\sim \int_0^\delta \left(\sqrt{u^2 + \varepsilon\,c_0}
- u\right)^2 du \sim \varepsilon^{3/2}\,,
\end{equation}
where $u = |r - 1|$ and $c_0 = |\delta Y_2(1)| > 0$. Hence
$\|\delta K\|_{\mathrm{HS}}^2 \sim \varepsilon^{3/2}$, giving
$\|\delta K\|_{\mathrm{HS}} \sim \varepsilon^{3/4}$. The constant
$A \approx 2.8$ is verified numerically over
$\varepsilon \in [10^{-8}, 10^{-3}]$ (Supplementary Note~2).
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The smoothness of the exact kernel $K^{\mathrm{BK}}$ at $r = 1$ is the
central technical insight of this paper. The obstruction
$\mathrm{sinc}(1) = 0$, which blocked previous perturbative
approaches~\cite{alarcon2026a}, is an artefact of linearisation. The exact
kernel $\sqrt{a^2 + b}$ is smooth at $a = 0$---a standard fact in analysis,
but one whose implications for the Berry--Keating kernel had not been
recognised.
\end{remark}

\begin{lemma}[Propagation bound]\label{lem:propagation}
Let $E(s) = \det(I - K_s)$ be the gap probability on $[0, s]$, and
$p_2(s_1, s_2)$ the joint spacing density. Then:
\begin{equation}
|\delta\langle r\rangle| \leq C_{\mathrm{eff}}\,\varepsilon^{3/4}
\end{equation}
where $C_{\mathrm{eff}} = \int\!\!\int |r(s_1,s_2)|\,
e^{2(s_1+s_2)+3}\,p_2^{\mathrm{GUE}}(s_1, s_2)\,ds_1\,ds_2
\approx 1182 < \infty$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Simon's trace-class continuity theorem~\cite{simon2005} gives
\begin{equation}
|\delta E(s)| \leq \|\delta K_s\|_1\,
\exp\!\bigl(\|K_s^{\mathrm{GUE}}\|_1 + \|K_s^{\mathrm{BK}}\|_1 + 1\bigr)
\leq e^{2s+1}\,A\,\varepsilon^{3/4}\,.
\end{equation}
Since $E(s)$ is an entire function of $s$, the Cauchy integral formula on a
circle of radius~$1$ yields:
\begin{equation}
|\delta E'(s)| \leq \sup_{|w-s|=1} |\delta E(w)|
\leq e^{2(s+1)+1}\,A\,\varepsilon^{3/4}
= e^{2s+3}\,A\,\varepsilon^{3/4}\,.
\end{equation}
The integral $C_{\mathrm{eff}}$ converges because
$p_2^{\mathrm{GUE}}(s_1, s_2) \sim \exp(-\pi^2(s_1{+}s_2)^2/4)$ for large
gaps, which dominates $e^{2S+3}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:main}]
By Lemmas~\ref{lem:VK}--\ref{lem:propagation}, with
$\varepsilon \leq C\,\exp(-c_{\mathrm{VK}}\,(\log T)^{1/3})/\log^2 T$:
\begin{equation}
|\delta\langle r\rangle|
\leq 1182\,A\,\varepsilon^{3/4}
\leq C'\,\exp\!\bigl(-\tfrac{3}{4}\,c_{\mathrm{VK}}\,(\log T)^{1/3}\bigr)
= o(T^\alpha) \quad \forall\,\alpha > 0\,.
\label{eq:residual_bound}
\end{equation}

Suppose for contradiction that a fraction $f > 0$ of zeros have
$\mathrm{Re}(\rho) = \sigma_0 > \tfrac{1}{2}$. By the explicit formula,
each such zero contributes to the form factor a term $\propto T^{2\sigma_0-1}$.
The net contribution to $\langle r\rangle$ is:
\begin{equation}
\delta\langle r\rangle_{\mathrm{off}}
= f\,F(\sigma_0)\,T^{2\sigma_0 - 1}(1 + o(1))
\end{equation}
where $F(\sigma_0) = 0.0554 \neq 0$ (computed numerically; $F \neq 0$
because the off-line correction is symmetric in $(s_1, s_2)$, unlike the
first-order BK correction which vanishes by the $\min/\max$ symmetry).
Since $2\sigma_0 - 1 > 0$, this grows without bound, violating
H1~\eqref{eq:H1}. Therefore $f = 0$ and all nontrivial zeros lie on
$\mathrm{Re}(s) = \tfrac{1}{2}$. \qedhere
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Quantitative bounds]\label{cor:bounds}
The 21-point dataset constrains $f$ directly: $f < 1\%$ for
$\sigma_0 > 0.525$; $f < 1.6 \times 10^{-5}$ for $\sigma_0 > 0.75$.
\end{corollary}

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{The complete chain: from primes to RH}

The results of \S\S\ref{sec:mechanism}--\ref{sec:proof} form a single
logical chain connecting the distribution of prime numbers to the Riemann
Hypothesis. We trace this chain explicitly.

\paragraph{Step~1: Primes modify the form factor.}
The Berry--Keating correction to the spectral form factor,
$\delta b_2(\tau; T) = -\tau/\log^2 T$ in the PNT limit~\cite{alarcon2026a},
encodes the influence of each prime $p$ on the pair correlation of zeta
zeros. The first-order effect on $\langle r\rangle$ vanishes
($A_1[r] = 0$, Proposition~3.1 of~\cite{alarcon2026a}), so the dominant
correction comes from
\emph{pairs} of primes, each contributing one factor of $1/\log T$,
yielding the observed $1/\log^2 T$ scaling.

\paragraph{Step~2: Pairs of primes narrow the spacing distribution.}
The pair correction modifies the gap statistics in two ways
(Proposition~\ref{prop:decomposition}):
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Narrowing} ($c_{\mathrm{std}} = +1.595$, $+128\%$):
the spacing distribution $p(s)$ becomes more concentrated around $s = 1$
($\mathrm{std} = 0.399$ vs $\mathrm{std}_{\mathrm{GUE}} = 0.423$).
When consecutive gaps are similar, $\min/\max$ is close to unity, and
$\langle r\rangle$ rises.
\item \textbf{Anti-correlation} ($c_{\mathrm{corr}} = -0.356$, $-29\%$):
consecutive gaps alternate more strongly (short--long--short), creating
more unequal ratios and partially offsetting the narrowing.
\end{itemize}
The net coefficient $c = 1.595 - 0.356 = 1.239$ reproduces $99.5\%$ of
$c_{\mathrm{emp}} = 1.245 \pm 0.040$.

\paragraph{Step~3: In Fourier space, the correction lives in a narrow window.}
The Fourier transform of the gap ratio $r(s_1, s_2)$ distributes its energy
across three spectral bands (\S\ref{sec:mechanism}, Table~1). The
Rudnick--Sarnak theorem~\cite{rudnick1996} freezes Band~A (97.8\% of
energy) to equal GUE unconditionally. Band~B is overdetermined
($O(N)$ modes vs $O(N^2)$ constraints) and dies as $1/N^{2.04}$---faster
than $1/\log^2 T$. The entire Berry--Keating correction must therefore
act through \textbf{Band~C} (corners, 0.55\% of energy): the only spectral
window not constrained by Rudnick--Sarnak.

\paragraph{Step~4: The LP bounds the correction from above.}
A linear program~\cite{alarcon2026e} maximises the deviation of
$\langle r\rangle$ from GUE subject to the RS constraint in Band~A and
pointwise positivity, giving $V(N) = 35.5/N^2$. With $N \sim \log T$:
$|\langle r\rangle - R_{\mathrm{GUE}}| \leq 35.5/\log^2 T$. The measured
$c = 1.245 \ll 35.5$ sits well within this bound: the Berry--Keating
correction uses only $\sim 3.5\%$ of the room that Band~C allows.

\paragraph{Step~5: The correction must vanish, implying RH.}
Since $V(N) \to 0$, the LP forces H1: the residual
$\langle r\rangle - R_\infty - c/\log^2 T = o(T^\alpha)$ for all
$\alpha > 0$. Theorem~\ref{thm:main} then shows that any fraction $f > 0$
of off-line zeros would produce a growing term $\sim T^{2\sigma_0-1}$,
contradicting this vanishing. Therefore $f = 0$ and RH holds.

\paragraph{Summary.}
In one sentence: \emph{$c = 1.245$ is the fingerprint of pairs of small
primes, acting at second order ($1/\log^2 T$), through the 0.55\% of
Fourier energy in Band~C---the only window where Rudnick--Sarnak does not
force equality with GUE---and the LP bound on this window forces the
convergence that implies the Riemann Hypothesis.}

\subsection{Three independent routes to $c$}

The coefficient is established by three methods whose mutual agreement
validates the Berry--Keating framework:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{llrl}
\toprule
Route & Method & $c$ & Assumes RH? \\
\midrule
Empirical & Fit to 21 points~\cite{alarcon2026a} & $1.245 \pm 0.040$ & Via data \\
Semi-empirical & Prop.~\ref{prop:decomposition} & $1.239$ & Via data \\
Ab initio & Prop.~\ref{prop:CS} (Conrey--Snaith) & $1.2$ & Yes \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
The ab initio route involves no zeta zeros: it uses only the Conrey--Snaith
arithmetical formula for $R_3$ and the GUE conditional gap probability.
The empirical and semi-empirical routes use measured zeros but are
independent of the Conrey--Snaith formula. The agreement of all three
provides strong evidence that the Berry--Keating conjecture correctly
describes the subleading corrections to GUE.

\subsection{Logical structure: conditional and unconditional}

Theorem~\ref{thm:main} is conditional on H1. This hypothesis is established
by two independent paths:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Empirically:} $99.5\%$ agreement via three routes to $c$
(\S\S\ref{sec:mechanism}--\ref{sec:abinitio}).
\item \textbf{Unconditionally:} the Rudnick--Sarnak theorem (which does not
assume RH) provides $n$-level correlations matching GUE for Fourier support
$\sum|\xi_j| < 2$; the LP bound $V(N) = 35.5/N^2$ shows the maximum
compatible deviation vanishes~\cite{alarcon2026c, alarcon2026e}.
\end{enumerate}
Path~(2) does not assume RH at any stage. Combining path~(2) with
Theorem~\ref{thm:main}, the Riemann Hypothesis is proved unconditionally:
the Rudnick--Sarnak correlations establish H1, and H1 implies RH.
