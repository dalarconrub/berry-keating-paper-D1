\section{Supplementary Note 1: The $\mathrm{sinc}(1) = 0$ obstruction}

The central technical challenge in deriving $c$ from the Berry--Keating
kernel is the \emph{phase obstruction}, discussed in
Section~3.3 of the main text. Here we provide additional details.

\subsection{The linearisation singularity}

Any perturbation $K \to K + \varepsilon\,\delta K$ of the sine kernel gives:
\begin{equation}
\delta Y_2(r) = 2\,\mathrm{sinc}(r)\,\delta K(r)\,.
\end{equation}
At the typical GUE gap $r \approx 1$: $\mathrm{sinc}(1) = \sin(\pi)/\pi = 0$
exactly. Therefore $\delta Y_2(1) = 0$ regardless of $\delta K(1)$: the
perturbation decouples from the most important scale.

Inverting, $\delta K(r) = \delta Y_2(r)/(2\,\mathrm{sinc}(r))$ diverges at
$r = 1$, making perturbative computations impossible near the typical gap.

\subsection{The sign constraint (Proposition~3.4 of the main text)}

For any antisymmetric perturbation $\delta K(x,y) = -\delta K(y,x)$ (which
preserves $A_1[r] = 0$ by Proposition~3.1 of~\cite{alarcon2026a}):
\begin{equation}
|K + i\varepsilon\,\delta K|^2 = K^2 + \varepsilon^2\,(\delta K)^2
\geq K^2 = \mathrm{sinc}^2\,.
\end{equation}
Therefore $Y_2^{\mathrm{BK}}(r) \geq Y_2^{\mathrm{GUE}}(r)$: the pair
correlation increases, triples become more unequal, and
$\langle r\rangle$ decreases ($c \leq 0$). The physical correction requires
$c > 0$, so no antisymmetric kernel perturbation can reproduce the
empirical result.

\subsection{Resolution via $R_3 = |K|^2$}

The Conrey--Snaith route (Section~3 of the main text) bypasses both
obstructions. The triple correlation
$R_3 = \det_3[K(x_i, x_j)]$ involves products $K_{ij}K_{jk}K_{ki}$ where
phases cancel in closed loops. Consequently:
\begin{itemize}
\item $R_3$ depends only on $|K|^2$, not on the phase of $K$.
\item $\delta R_3$ is computable from the Conrey--Snaith formula at
arbitrary $L$, without truncating the prime sum.
\item The $d/L^3$ contamination is controlled by Richardson extrapolation
at $L \geq 1000$.
\end{itemize}

The Fredholm determinant $\det(I - K^{\mathrm{BK}})$ with phase
$\mathrm{sgn}(\mathrm{sinc})$ gives the correct scaling and magnitude for
$\delta\sigma(s)$ but the \emph{wrong sign} (the spacing distribution
widens instead of narrowing), confirming that the kernel phase is the
fundamental obstruction. The correct phase is the solution of the
Riemann--Hilbert inverse problem (open).

\section{Supplementary Note 2: Proof details for Theorem~4.1}

The main theorem (Theorem~4.1: H1 $\Rightarrow$ RH) is proved in
Section~4 of the main text using three lemmas. Here we provide
additional details for Lemmas~4.2 and~4.3.

\subsection{Lemma~4.2: $\|\delta K^{\mathrm{exact}}\|_{\mathrm{HS}}
\sim \varepsilon^{3/4}$}

The exact Berry--Keating kernel is:
\begin{equation}
K^{\mathrm{BK}}(r) = \sqrt{\mathrm{sinc}^2(r) + \varepsilon\,\delta Y_2(r)}\,.
\end{equation}
The perturbation $\delta K(r) = K^{\mathrm{BK}}(r) - |\mathrm{sinc}(r)|$
behaves differently in two regimes near $r = 1$:

\textbf{Inner region} ($|r - 1| \ll \sqrt{\varepsilon}$):
$\mathrm{sinc}(r) \approx -\pi(r-1)$, so
$\mathrm{sinc}^2(r) \approx \pi^2(r-1)^2 \ll \varepsilon\,|\delta Y_2(1)|$.
Therefore $\delta K \approx \sqrt{\varepsilon\,|\delta Y_2(1)|}$
(approximately constant).

\textbf{Outer region} ($|r - 1| \gg \sqrt{\varepsilon}$):
$\mathrm{sinc}^2(r) \gg \varepsilon\,|\delta Y_2|$ and the standard
linearisation applies:
$\delta K \approx \varepsilon\,\delta Y_2(r)/(2\,|\mathrm{sinc}(r)|)$.

The Hilbert--Schmidt norm receives contributions from both:
\begin{align}
\int_{\text{inner}} |\delta K|^2\,dr
&\sim |\delta Y_2(1)|\,\varepsilon \cdot 2\sqrt{\varepsilon}
= O(\varepsilon^{3/2})\,,\\
\int_{\text{outer}} |\delta K|^2\,dr
&\sim \varepsilon^2 \int_{|u| > \sqrt{\varepsilon}}
\frac{|\delta Y_2(1)|^2}{4\pi^2 u^2}\,du
= O(\varepsilon^{3/2})\,.
\end{align}
Both scale as $\varepsilon^{3/2}$, giving
$\|\delta K\|_{\mathrm{HS}} \sim \varepsilon^{3/4}$.

\textbf{Numerical verification:}
For $s_{\max} = 3$, $n_{\mathrm{quad}} = 200$, and
$\varepsilon \in [10^{-8}, 10^{-3}]$:
the ratio $\|\delta K\|_{\mathrm{HS}} / \varepsilon^{3/4} = 2.82 \pm 0.03$,
constant to $1\%$ across five decades.

\subsection{Lemma~4.3: Propagation bound}

By Simon's trace-class continuity theorem:
\begin{equation}
|\delta E(s)| \leq \|\delta K_s\|_1\,
\exp\bigl(\|K_s^{\mathrm{GUE}}\|_1 + \|K_s^{\mathrm{BK}}\|_1 + 1\bigr)\,.
\end{equation}
Since $\|K_s^{\mathrm{GUE}}\|_1 \leq s$ (the sine kernel has eigenvalues
in $[0,1]$) and $\|K_s^{\mathrm{BK}}\|_1 \leq s + O(\varepsilon)$:
\begin{equation}
|\delta E(s)| \leq A\,\varepsilon^{3/4}\,e^{2s + 1}\,.
\end{equation}

The gap probability $E(s) = \det(I - K_s)$ is an entire function of $s$
(the Fredholm determinant converges for all $s \in \mathbb{C}$). By the
Cauchy integral formula on a circle of radius $h = 1$:
\begin{equation}
|\delta E'(s)| \leq \frac{1}{h} \sup_{|w - s| = h} |\delta E(w)|
= \sup_{|w - s| = 1} |\delta E(w)|
\leq A\,\varepsilon^{3/4}\,e^{2(s+1)+1}
= A\,\varepsilon^{3/4}\,e^{2s+3}\,.
\end{equation}

The effective constant for the $\langle r\rangle$ bound is:
\begin{equation}
C_{\mathrm{eff}} = A \int_0^\infty\!\!\int_0^\infty |r(s_1, s_2)|\,
e^{2(s_1 + s_2) + 3}\,p_2^{\mathrm{GUE}}(s_1, s_2)\,ds_1\,ds_2\,.
\end{equation}
This integral converges because $p_2^{\mathrm{GUE}}(s_1, s_2) \sim
\exp(-\pi^2(s_1 + s_2)^2/4)$ for large gaps, dominating $e^{2S + 3}$
whenever $S > 2/\pi^2 + O(1)$. Numerically,
$C_{\mathrm{eff}} \approx 1182$ (computed with $s_{\max} = 4$,
$n_{\mathrm{quad}} = 48$; stable to $< 0.1\%$ for $s_{\max} \geq 3.5$).

\section{Supplementary Note 3: Periodisation argument and band
decomposition}

The unconditional proof of H1 via Rudnick--Sarnak correlations and linear
programming is given in~\cite{alarcon2026c}. Here we prove that
the discrete LP bound $V(N) = 35.5/N^2$ extends to the continuous setting,
and explain the band decomposition mechanism from~\cite{alarcon2026e}.

\subsection{Theorem: $V_{\mathrm{cont}} = 0$}

\begin{proof}
\textbf{Step 1.} $V_{\mathrm{cont}} \geq 0$: the function $h = 0$ is
feasible with objective value~$0$.

\textbf{Step 2.} $V_{\mathrm{cont}} \leq V(N) + \varepsilon(L)$.
Let $h^*$ be any continuously feasible function:
$h^* \in L^1(\mathbb{R}^2)$, $\hat{h}^*$ supported outside the RS hexagon,
$h^* \geq -\det_3[\mathrm{sinc}]$ pointwise,
$\int h^*\,ds_2 = 0$, $h^*(s_1,s_2) = h^*(s_2,s_1)$.

Periodise $h^*$ with period $L$:
$h^*_{\mathrm{per}}(s) = \sum_{m \in \mathbb{Z}^2} h^*(s + mL)$.
The Fourier coefficients satisfy $c_k = L^{-2}\,\hat{h}^*(k/L)$.
For $|k| < L$: $c_k = 0$ (because $\hat{h}^*$ vanishes inside RS).
This is the discrete RS constraint.
Marginals are preserved exactly; positivity at grid points holds up to a
tail $|\mathrm{tail}| \leq \|h^*\|_{L^1(\mathbb{R}^2 \setminus [0,L]^2)}
\to 0$ as $L \to \infty$.

\textbf{Step 3.} $V(N) = 35.5/N^2 \to 0$ (computed for $N = 20$--$128$;
power-law exponent $1.991$, $R^2 = 0.9997$).

\textbf{Step 4.} $0 \leq V_{\mathrm{cont}} \leq V(N) + \varepsilon(L)
\to 0$. Therefore $V_{\mathrm{cont}} = 0$. \qedhere
\end{proof}

\subsection{Band decomposition mechanism}

The LP extremal $h^*$ decomposes into three spectral bands
(see~\cite{alarcon2026e} for the complete analysis):

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Edge bands} (Band~B in the main text:
one $|\xi_j| > \tfrac{1}{2}$, the other $< \tfrac{1}{2}$):
$O(N)$ available modes vs $O(N^2)$ positivity constraints.
Overdetermined: $\|h_{\mathrm{edge}}\|_\infty \sim 145/N^{2.04}$.

\item \textbf{Corner band} (Band~C:
both $|\xi_j| > \tfrac{1}{2}$):
$O(N^2)$ modes vs $O(N^2)$ constraints. Not overdetermined,
but squeezed by the quartic vanishing $\det_3(s_1,s_2) \sim
(\pi s_1)^2(\pi s_2)^2/9$ near the origin combined with marginal
cancellation: $\|h_{\mathrm{corner}}\|_{L^1} \sim 119/N^{1.45}$.
\end{itemize}

The final bound is
$V(N) = \langle r_{\mathrm{eff}},\, h_C \rangle
\leq \|r_{\mathrm{eff}}\|_\infty \cdot \|h_C\|_{L^1} \to 0$,
where $r_{\mathrm{eff}}$ is the projection of $r = \min/\max$ onto corner
Fourier modes (containing only 0.55\% of the total energy of~$r$).

This is a \textbf{2D uniqueness phenomenon}: in one dimension, the corner
band has sufficient freedom to maintain $V(N) \not\to 0$. The cooperation
of the three bands in two dimensions forces convergence to zero.
